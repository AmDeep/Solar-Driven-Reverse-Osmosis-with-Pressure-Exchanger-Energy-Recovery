1. Introduction and Engineering Motivation

Concentration polarisation (CP) is the accumulation of rejected solute (NaCl) at the membrane surface due to the competition between convective transport toward the membrane (at the permeate flux rate J) and diffusive back-transport away from it. It is the single most important mass-transfer limitation in reverse osmosis — it raises the effective osmotic pressure at the membrane face, reduces net driving pressure, and if severe, triggers scaling.

For a 20,000 L/day SWRO system operating at 45% recovery and 60 bar, understanding CP is essential for:

  • Verifying that the 60 bar operating pressure provides adequate net driving pressure (NDP) even at the elevated surface concentration
  • Specifying the minimum crossflow velocity to keep CP within safe bounds
  • Quantifying the additional osmotic pressure load on the membrane (and hence the HP pump)

This study presents two complementary approaches: (1) the classical film-theory analytical solution via the Graetz–Lévêque analogy, and (2) a full 2D finite-difference numerical simulation of the convection–diffusion equation in the feed channel.

2. Physical Model and Governing Equations

2.1 Geometry

The RO feed channel is modelled as a 2D rectangular duct of length L = 0.85 m (one Filmtec SW30XHR-400i element) and height H = 1 mm (representative spacer channel thickness). The membrane occupies the bottom wall (y = 0). The top wall is impermeable.

2.2 Governing PDE

The convection–diffusion equation for salt concentration C(x, y, t) in the feed channel:

∂C/∂t + u(y)·∂C/∂x + v(y)·∂C/∂y = D·∇²C where: u(y) = 6U·(y/H)·(1 − y/H) [Poiseuille parabolic profile, fully developed] v(y) ≈ −J·(1 − y/H) [suction-induced transverse velocity, decays away from membrane] D = 1.61 × 10⁻⁹ m²/s [NaCl binary diffusivity at 25°C, CRC Handbook] U = 0.10 m/s [mean crossflow velocity] J = 5.56 × 10⁻⁶ m/s [permeate flux = 20 LMH ÷ 3600 s/h ÷ 1000 L/m³]

2.3 Boundary Conditions

Inlet (x = 0): C = C₀ = 35 g/L [uniform feed salinity] Outlet (x = L): ∂C/∂x = 0 [Neumann — fully developed] Top wall (y = H): ∂C/∂y = 0 [impermeable] Membrane (y = 0): ∂C/∂y = J·C/D [concentration polarisation BC] The membrane BC is derived from the salt mass balance at the membrane face: J·C_m = D·(∂C/∂y)|₀ → ∂C/∂y|₀ = J·C_m/D This states that the diffusive flux away from the membrane equals the convective salt flux toward it. Perfect rejection is assumed (C_permeate = 0), which is conservative for Filmtec SW30XHR-400i (99.75% rejection).

3. Analytical Solution: Graetz–Lévêque Film Theory

3.1 Derivation

For laminar duct flow with a developing concentration boundary layer, the Graetz–Nusselt mass-transfer analogy gives the local Sherwood number:

Sh_x = 1.85·(Re·Sc·d_h/x)^(1/3) [Lévêque 1928; see also Brian & Hales 1969] where: Re = U·d_h/ν = 0.10 × 0.002 / (8.9 × 10⁻⁷) = 225 [laminar, Re << 2300 ✓] Sc = ν/D = 8.9 × 10⁻⁷ / (1.61 × 10⁻⁹) = 553 [high Sc → thin concentration BL] d_h = 2H = 0.002 m [hydraulic diameter, parallel plates] Local mass-transfer coefficient: k_x = Sh_x · D / d_h Concentration polarisation factor (film-theory): CP = C_m/C_b = exp(J/k_x) [derived from steady-state boundary layer balance]

4. Numerical Solution: 2D Finite-Difference Method

4.1 Discretisation

Grid: Nx × Ny = 120 × 30 (x-direction × y-direction) Δx = L/119 = 0.85/119 = 7.14 × 10⁻³ m Δy = H/29 = 0.001/29 = 3.45 × 10⁻⁵ m Explicit forward Euler time-stepping: C[i,j]^(n+1) = C[i,j]^n + Δt · R[i,j]^n R[i,j] = −u_j · (∂C/∂x)_upwind − v_j · (∂C/∂y)_upwind + D · [(C[i+1,j] − 2C[i,j] + C[i-1,j])/Δx² + (C[i,j+1] − 2C[i,j] + C[i,j-1])/Δy²] Stability (von Neumann analysis): Δt < min(Δy²/(2D), Δx/U) Δt_diffusion = 0.4 × Δy²/D = 0.4 × (3.45e-5)²/1.61e-9 = 0.295 s Δt_convection = 0.4 × Δx/U = 0.4 × 7.14e-3/0.1 = 0.029 s ← limiting Δt_adopted = 0.029 s (convection CFL limited) Time to quasi-steady state: 5 × τ_diffusion = 5 × H²/(2D) = 5 × (0.001)²/(2×1.61e-9) = 1,553 s Number of time steps: 1,553 / 0.029 = 53,552 steps

4.2 Membrane boundary condition (explicit)

At j=0 (membrane, y=0): Discretise ∂C/∂y|₀ = J·C_m/D using one-sided first-order difference: (C[i,1] − C[i,0]) / Δy = J·C[i,0] / D C[i,0] = C[i,1] / (1 − J·Δy/D) = C[i,1] / (1 − 5.56e-6 × 3.45e-5 / 1.61e-9) = C[i,1] / (1 − 0.119) = C[i,1] / 0.881 = 1.135 × C[i,1] This local amplification factor (~1.135 per cell) builds up over many cells until balanced by diffusion — giving the steady-state CP profile.

Why does the FD match the analytical? Both models use the same governing physics (convection–diffusion with CP boundary condition). The Graetz–Lévêque solution assumes a developing concentration boundary layer in the entry region (Lévêque approximation), which is valid here because Sc=553 means the concentration boundary layer is much thinner than the momentum boundary layer. The FD confirms this is a valid regime.

5. Engineering Significance

5.1 Net driving pressure with CP

Without CP: π_feed = 28.4 bar (van't Hoff at 35 g/L) NDP = ΔP − π_feed = 60 − 28.4 = 31.6 bar With CP (CP = 1.49): π_membrane = 28.4 × 1.49 = 42.3 bar NDP_effective = 60 − 42.3 = 17.7 bar CP therefore reduces NDP by 44% vs. the bulk-concentration value. The design flux of 20 LMH requires NDP > ~10–15 bar (estimated from Filmtec datasheet), so 17.7 bar provides a safe margin — but only just. This confirms that crossflow velocity must be maintained ≥ 0.1 m/s.

Notek ∝ U^(1/3) via Sh ∝ Re^(1/3). Doubling U increases k by only 26% (diminishing returns). But pressure drop ∝ U² — so doubling U quadruples pumping energy in the feed channel.

5.3 Temperature sensitivity

At T = 35°C (warm coastal site): D_NaCl ≈ 1.90 × 10⁻⁹ m²/s (+18% vs 25°C, Stokes-Einstein) ν ≈ 7.3 × 10⁻⁷ m²/s (−18% vs 25°C) k ∝ D^(2/3) · ν^(−1/3) → k_35°C ≈ 14.8 × (1.18)^(2/3) × (0.82)^(-1/3) ≈ 14.8 × 1.12 × 1.07 ≈ 17.8 × 10⁻⁶ m/s CP_35°C = exp(J/k) = exp(5.56/17.8) ≈ 1.37 (improved vs 25°C) Counter-effect: osmotic pressure also rises ~2%/°C: π_35°C ≈ 28.4 × 1.02^10 = 34.7 bar → π_membrane ≈ 34.7 × 1.37 = 47.5 bar NDP_35°C ≈ 60 − 47.5 = 12.5 bar (tighter margin — warmer sites need care)

What I am uncertain about in this CFD study: The 2D model assumes a fully-developed Poiseuille profile and no spacer effects. Real RO elements use tricot spacers that create 3D secondary flows (Dean vortices, jet impingement). These could raise the effective mass-transfer coefficient by 1.5–3×, meaning actual CP is 1.2–1.35 — better than the 1.49 computed here. The analytical model is therefore a conservative (safe) upper bound for design purposes. To reduce this uncertainty: obtain experimental k values from the membrane manufacturer (Filmtec) at the specific operating conditions, or run 3D DNS/LES of the spacer geometry.
