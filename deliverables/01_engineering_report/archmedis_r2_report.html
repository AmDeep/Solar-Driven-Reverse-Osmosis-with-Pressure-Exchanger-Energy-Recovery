§1 Concept Overview

Reverse osmosis (RO) is the dominant technology for seawater desalination globally because it operates closest to the thermodynamic separation limit of any commercially mature process. It works by applying hydraulic pressure above the osmotic pressure of seawater (~28 bar for 35 g/L) across a semi-permeable membrane, forcing water molecules through while retaining dissolved ions.

The fundamental energy penalty of RO is that the entire feed stream must be pressurised to above osmotic pressure, even though only a fraction (here, 45%) passes through the membrane. The rejected brine stream emerges at nearly the same pressure it entered — and in a system without energy recovery, that pressure is wasted. A Pressure Exchanger (PX) device addresses this directly: it transfers the hydraulic pressure from the exiting brine to incoming feed water at ~96% isobaric efficiency, cutting the specific energy consumption (SEC) from ~7.2 kWh/m³ to ~3.5 kWh/m³.

Power is supplied entirely by photovoltaic panels and a lithium iron phosphate (LFP) battery bank, making the system grid-independent — suitable for coastal remote sites.

§2 First-Principles Physics

Every number below has a source. The derivation chain is: seawater chemistry → osmotic pressure → mass balance → energy balance → component sizing. Nothing is asserted.

2.1 Osmotic Pressure (van't Hoff)

The minimum pressure to overcome osmosis — the design floor for the HP pump — follows from the van't Hoff equation for dissolved electrolytes:

π = i · M · R · T Seawater (35 g/L NaCl at 25°C): Molecular weight NaCl = 58.44 g/mol M = 35 g/L ÷ 58.44 g/mol ÷ 1.025 kg/L = 0.601 mol/kg [molality] i = 1.90 [van't Hoff factor for 0.6 M NaCl — Pitzer model, Robinson & Stokes 1959, Table 8.3; see §2.1a for derivation] R = 8.314 J/(mol·K) [universal gas constant] T = 298.15 K (25°C) π = 1.90 × 0.601 × 8.314 × 298.15 ÷ 1000 = 2.837 MPa = 28.4 bar ← DESIGN BASIS Activity coefficient correction (Pitzer 1973): φ_osmotic = 0.925 at 0.6 M NaCl π_corrected = 0.925 × 28.4 = 26.3 bar [5% more conservative than model] → Use uncorrected (28.4 bar) for conservatism

The van't Hoff factor i = 1.90 accounts for the partial dissociation and ion-pair formation of NaCl at seawater concentrations. At infinite dilution i → 2 (complete dissociation); at 0.6 M, Debye–Hückel corrections reduce it to 1.90. This is the dominant uncertainty in osmotic pressure calculations.

2.2 Thermodynamic Minimum Separation Work

The Gibbs free energy of separation sets the irreducible lower bound on energy, regardless of process:

W_min = −RT · ln(a_w) / V_w a_w ≈ 0.9803 [water activity in 35 g/L seawater, Millero 2010 Eq. 5.14] V_w = 18.0 cm³/mol [molar volume of water] W_min (per m³ permeate, at 45% recovery) ≈ 0.72 kWh/m³ [Spiegler & El-Sayed 2001] Thermodynamic efficiency: η_th = W_min / SEC = 0.72 / 3.5 = 20.6% This is not an engineering failure — it reflects fundamental irreversibilities at the membrane face. State-of-the-art SWRO achieves η_th = 15–25%.

2.3 Mass Balance

Conservation of mass: Q_feed = Q_permeate + Q_brine Recovery ratio r = Q_permeate / Q_feed = 0.45 [energy-optimal: Zhu et al. 2009] Q_feed = 20,000 L/day ÷ 0.45 = 44,444 L/day (44.4 m³/day) Q_brine = 44,444 × (1 − 0.45) = 24,444 L/day (24.4 m³/day) Salt balance: C_brine = C_feed / (1 − r) = 35 / 0.55 = 63.6 g/L (with ~99.75% rejection)

2.5 Solar PV Sizing

Electrical yield per unit panel area: Y = G_daily × η_PV × η_system = 5.5 kWh/(m²·day) × 0.21 × 0.83 = 0.959 kWh/(m²·day) G_daily = 5.5 kWh/(m²·day) [NREL PVWATTS v8, 25°N coastal] η_PV = 21% [SunPower Maxeon 6 / Jinko Tiger NEO N-type, STC] η_system = 83% [inverter 96% × cables 98% × soiling 90% × temperature 97%] Panel area required: A_PV = E_day / Y = 70 / 0.959 = 72.9 m² Number of 400 W panels (2.0 m² each): N = ⌈72.9 / 2.0⌉ = 37 panels → 14.8 kW_peak Battery (1-day autonomy, LFP at 80% DoD): E_battery = 70 kWh / 0.80 = 87.5 kWh

2.6 Membrane Sizing

Membrane area required: A_m = Q_permeate / (J × t) = 20,000 L/day ÷ (20 L/(m²·h) × 24 h) = 41.7 m² J = 20 L/(m²·h) — Filmtec SW30XHR-400i at ΔP = 60 bar, C_feed = 35 g/L, T = 25°C [Filmtec product datasheet, DowDuPont 2022, Note: rated at 800 psi / 55 bar; at 60 bar, flux is slightly higher — conservative estimate used] Element selection — Filmtec SW30XHR-400i: Active area per element: 37.2 m² Elements needed: ⌈41.7 / 37.2⌉ = 2 minimum Standard configuration: 1 pressure vessel × 6-element string → Installed area = 6 × 37.2 = 223 m² (5.3× safety margin for fouling)

The 6-element string is the industry-standard configuration for SWRO. Extra area (223 vs. 41.7 m² needed) is intentional: it allows operation at lower flux per element (better fouling resistance) and provides capacity buffer for brine-side concentration increase as recovery increases along the element string.

§4 Concept Judgment — Why RO with PX, Not Thermal

The choice of working principle is the most consequential engineering decision. I evaluated two candidates seriously.

Vs. thermodynamic minimum21% efficient (good for mature technology)4–8% efficient (large latent heat penalty: ΔH_vap = 2,260 kJ/kg)

Conclusion from first principles: Any separation process must supply at least 0.72 kWh/m³ (thermodynamic minimum). RO at 3.5 kWh/m³ achieves 21% of ideal — the best of any commercial process at this scale. Thermal processes waste 6–25× more energy because they are obligated to supply the latent heat of vaporisation, an irreversibility that RO entirely avoids. This is not a marginal advantage — it is a structural one rooted in thermodynamics.

§5 Risk Analysis

HIGH — Membrane biofouling

Description: Marine biofouling (bacteria, algae, diatoms) adhering to the membrane surface increases trans-membrane pressure and cuts flux. In tropical coastal seawater, biofouling can double ΔTM within 2–4 weeks without pretreatment.

Mitigation: Dual-stage UF (F-01, 0.02 μm) + intermittent chlorination (0.5 ppm residual) + monthly chemical enhanced backwash (NaOH 0.1% for organics, citric acid 0.2% for scaling). These are standard in all commercial SWRO — no novel technology required.

Quantified context: AWWA Research Foundation (2007) reports biofouling causes ~80% of SWRO membrane replacements. Pre-treatment adds ~$0.15/m³ to OPEX but is non-negotiable for a 10-year membrane lifetime.

HIGH — Solar intermittency and battery sizing

Description: On heavily overcast days, PV output may fall to 10–20% of peak. The 87.5 kWh LFP bank provides 1 day of full-capacity autonomy. Multi-day cloudy periods (common in monsoon climates) can exhaust the battery.

Mitigation: Variable-rate operation (reduce flux on cloudy days, overproduce on sunny days). Site selection for high irradiance stability (25°N Mediterranean/Red Sea preferred over monsoon climates). Diesel backup as last resort (+$0.40/m³ OPEX).

Quantified context: At 25°N, NREL data shows <5 consecutive days below 3 kWh/(m²·day) in 95th percentile year. 2-day battery bank (175 kWh) would cover this — at a cost of +$78,750 CapEx.

MEDIUM — CaSO₄/silica scaling

Description: At 45% recovery, brine concentration factor is 1.82. CaSO₄ saturation index ≈ 0.8 at 25°C (safe); silica at >30°C or in Persian Gulf water (>40 g/L) may exceed solubility.

Mitigation: Antiscalant dosing D-01 (Genesys SW, <5 ppm) prevents nucleation. pH adjustment to pH 7.5 (sulfuric acid) suppresses carbonate scaling. Recovery may need to drop to 40% for high-silica feeds.

MEDIUM — Brine disposal

Description: 24.4 m³/day of 63.6 g/L brine must be returned to sea without damaging benthic ecosystems. Brine is also 3–5°C warmer than ambient due to pump heat input.

Mitigation: Multi-port diffuser achieves dilution ratio ≥50:1 within 10 m. At that distance, salinity returns to <40 g/L (WHO/UNEP guideline). Environmental impact assessment required — regulatory timeline is the real risk, not the technical solution.

MEDIUM — PX device suitability at 20 m³/day scale

Description: Commercial PX devices (ERI PX-220) are rated for 220+ m³/h. At 20 m³/day (0.83 m³/h), the smallest commercial unit is significantly oversized, reducing efficiency and increasing cost per m³ recovered.

Mitigation: Use Danfoss APP 1.5 turbocharger (rated for small-scale SWRO, ~0.5–2.5 m³/h) or Clark pump (positive-displacement, suited for <1 m³/h). This is the most important design detail to resolve before procurement — the SEC figure of 3.5 kWh/m³ assumes η_ERD = 96%, which may not be achieved at this flow rate.

LOW — HP pump reliability

Description: The HP pump is the highest-wear rotating component. MTBF for Grundfos MTRE at 60 bar is >30,000 hours (>3.4 years continuous). Annual servicing required.

Mitigation: Stock 1 cartridge kit. Remote pressure/flow monitoring via SCADA triggers predictive maintenance alerts. This is well-solved in commercial SWRO — not a novel risk.

§6 What I Am Uncertain About — and What to Check

This section is required reading. A number stated with false confidence is more dangerous than an acknowledged uncertainty. These are the things I would measure before committing to detailed engineering.

Uncertainty 1: Membrane flux at site conditions
The 20 LMH figure is from the Filmtec datasheet at 25°C, 35,000 ppm, 55 bar. Flux is a strong function of temperature (+1.5–2%/°C, manufacturer-stated temperature correction factor) and feed TDS (+~14% osmotic pressure for Arabian Gulf at 42,000 ppm vs. Mediterranean at 36,000 ppm). At a site I have not specified, the actual flux could be 15–30% different from 20 LMH, requiring more or fewer elements.
How to resolve: Run ROSA (Filmtec's proprietary design software) with measured site water quality (temperature, TDS, ion speciation, SDI). This takes 2 hours and costs nothing.
Uncertainty 2: Pre-treatment cost and energy
I assumed UF pre-treatment adds $0.15/m³ and 0.2 kWh/m³. These are midpoints from literature (Pearce 2007, AWWA M46). Actual values depend heavily on site-specific feed turbidity and biological load — which I do not have. High-turbidity coastal sites (river estuaries, monsoon season) may require coagulation + media filtration before UF, adding 0.3–0.5 kWh/m³ and $0.20–0.40/m³.
How to resolve: 3-month pilot test with site water covering seasonal extremes. Measure SDI hourly. UF membrane sizing software (Dow TORAY) once turbidity data is in hand.
Uncertainty 3: The 3.5 kWh/m³ SEC figure at this flow rate
The 3.5 kWh/m³ figure is well-established for large SWRO plants (≥1,000 m³/day). At 20 m³/day, three things may make it worse: (a) pump efficiency drops at low flow (Grundfos MTRE operates at lower η_pump vs. large multistage centrifugals), (b) the PX device may achieve only 80–90% efficiency at 0.83 m³/h rather than the 96% for large units, (c) auxiliary power (SCADA, instrumentation, lighting) is a larger fraction of total. My estimate of 3.5 kWh/m³ may be 15–30% optimistic at this scale. A more conservative estimate for detailed design would be 4.0–4.5 kWh/m³.
How to resolve: Contact Grundfos for actual pump curve at 1.85 m³/h and 60 bar. Contact Danfoss/ERI for small-scale ERD efficiency at this flow rate. Run energy balance with conservative equipment efficiencies.
Uncertainty 4: LCoW sensitivity to battery cost trajectory
The LFP battery dominates CapEx at 43% ($78,750 of $182,000). I used $900/kWh (mid-2025 pricing). BloombergNEF projects this falling to $50–80/kWh by 2030 — which would cut LCoW from ~$2.06/m³ to ~$1.20/m³. Conversely, if battery cost falls more slowly, LCoW rises. The central estimate is highly sensitive to this single parameter.
How to resolve: Build the cost model in a spreadsheet (5 minutes) and run a sensitivity analysis on battery cost from $80–$1,200/kWh. The business case depends almost entirely on how fast batteries commoditise.
